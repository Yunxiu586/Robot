### Kinodynamic Path Searching

#####  Primitives Generation

We first discuss the generation of **motion primitives** used in `Expand()`.

The **differential flatness** of quadrotor systems allows the trajectory to be represented by three independent 1-D time-parameterized polynomial functions
$$
\boldsymbol{p}(t) := \begin{bmatrix} p_x(t) & p_y(t) & p_z(t) \end{bmatrix}^{T}
，p_{\mu}(t) = \sum_{k=0}^{K} a_k t^k
$$

where
$$
\mu \in \{x,y,z\}
$$
From the view of quadrotor systems, this corresponds to a linear time-invariant (LTI) system. Define the state vector and control input as
$$
\boldsymbol{x}(t) :=
\begin{bmatrix}
\boldsymbol{p}(t)^{T} & \dot{\boldsymbol{p}}(t)^{T} & \cdots & \boldsymbol{p}^{(n-1)}(t)^{T}
\end{bmatrix}^{T}
\in \mathcal{X} \subset \mathbb{R}^{3n}
$$

$$
\boldsymbol{u}(t) := \boldsymbol{p}^{(n)}(t) \in \mathcal{U} := [-u_{\max}, u_{\max}]^3 \subset \mathbb{R}^3
$$

The state-space model is

$$
\dot{\boldsymbol{x}} = \mathbf{A}\boldsymbol{x} + \mathbf{B}\boldsymbol{u}
$$

$$
\mathbf{A} =
\begin{bmatrix}
\mathbf{0} & \mathbf{I}_3 & \mathbf{0} & \cdots & \mathbf{0} \\
\mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{I}_3 & \cdots & \mathbf{0} \\
\vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\
\mathbf{0} & \cdots & \cdots & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{I}_3 \\
\mathbf{0} & \cdots & \cdots & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0}
\end{bmatrix},
\mathbf{B} =
\begin{bmatrix}
\mathbf{0} \\
\mathbf{0} \\
\vdots \\
\mathbf{0} \\
\mathbf{I}_3
\end{bmatrix}
$$

For the linear time-invariant system

$$
\dot{\boldsymbol{x}}(t)
=
\mathbf{A}\boldsymbol{x}(t)
+
\mathbf{B}\boldsymbol{u}(t),
$$

where $\mathbf{A}$ and $\mathbf{B}$ are constant matrices, the state transition can be derived by using the matrix exponential. 

First, multiply both sides by $e^{-\mathbf{A}t}$
$$
e^{-\mathbf{A}t}\dot{\boldsymbol{x}}(t)
=
e^{-\mathbf{A}t}\mathbf{A}\boldsymbol{x}(t)
+
e^{-\mathbf{A}t}\mathbf{B}\boldsymbol{u}(t).
$$

Since

$$
\frac{d}{dt}
\left(
e^{-\mathbf{A}t}\boldsymbol{x}(t)
\right)
=
e^{-\mathbf{A}t}\dot{\boldsymbol{x}}(t)
-
e^{-\mathbf{A}t}\mathbf{A}\boldsymbol{x}(t),
$$

we obtain

$$
\frac{d}{dt}
\left(
e^{-\mathbf{A}t}\boldsymbol{x}(t)
\right)
=
e^{-\mathbf{A}t}\mathbf{B}\boldsymbol{u}(t).
$$

Integrating both sides from $0$ to $t$ gives

$$
e^{-\mathbf{A}t}\boldsymbol{x}(t)
-
\boldsymbol{x}(0)
=
\int_0^t
e^{-\mathbf{A}\tau}
\mathbf{B}\boldsymbol{u}(\tau)
\,d\tau.
$$

Therefore,

$$
\boldsymbol{x}(t)
=
e^{\mathbf{A}t}\boldsymbol{x}(0)
+
e^{\mathbf{A}t}
\int_0^t
e^{-\mathbf{A}\tau}
\mathbf{B}\boldsymbol{u}(\tau)
\,d\tau.
$$

The **complete solution of the state equation** is
$$
\boldsymbol{x}(t) = e^{\mathbf{A}t}\boldsymbol{x}(0) + \int_{0}^{t} e^{\mathbf{A}(t-\tau)} \mathbf{B}\boldsymbol{u}(\tau)\, d\tau
$$

In `Expand()`, given the current quadrotor state, a set of discretized control inputs $\mathcal{U}_D \subset \mathcal{U}$ is applied for duration $\tau$. In practice $n=2$, which corresponds to a double integrator. Each axis is uniformly discretized as

$$
[-u_{\max}, u_{\max}]
\rightarrow
\left\{
-u_{\max},
-\frac{r-1}{r}u_{\max},
\cdots,
\frac{r-1}{r}u_{\max},
u_{\max}
\right\}
$$

which results in $(2r+1)^3$ motion primitives.

The step is
$$
\Delta u = \frac{u_{\max}}{r}
$$

##### Actual Cost and Heuristic Cost

The objective is to find a trajectory that is **optimal in time and control cost**. The **trajectory cost** is 

$$
\mathcal{J}(T) = \int_{0}^{T} \|\boldsymbol{u}(t)\|^2 dt + \rho T
$$

For a motion primitive generated by a constant discretized input $\boldsymbol{u}(t)=\boldsymbol{u}_d$ and duration $\tau$, the **edge cost** is
$$
e_c = (\|\boldsymbol{u}_d\|^2 + \rho)\tau
$$

If the optimal path from the start state $\boldsymbol{x}_s$ to the current state $\boldsymbol{x}_c$ consists of $J$ primitives, the **actual cost** is

$$
g_c = \sum_{j=1}^{J} (\|u_{dj}\|^2 + \rho)\tau
$$

An admissible and informative **heuristic** is essential to speed up the searching as in A*. A **closed-form trajectory minimizing** $\mathcal{J}(T)$ from the current state $\boldsymbol{x}_c$ to the goal state $\boldsymbol{x}_g$ is computed by applying Pontryagin's minimum principle
$$
p_{\mu}^{*}(t)
=
\frac{1}{6}\alpha_{\mu}t^3
+
\frac{1}{2}\beta_{\mu}t^2
+
v_{\mu c}t
+
p_{\mu c}
$$

$$
\begin{bmatrix}
\alpha_{\mu} \\
\beta_{\mu}
\end{bmatrix}
=
\frac{1}{T^3}
\begin{bmatrix}
-12 & 6T \\
6T & -2T^2
\end{bmatrix}
\begin{bmatrix}
p_{\mu g} - p_{\mu c} - v_{\mu c}T \\
v_{\mu g} - v_{\mu c}
\end{bmatrix}
$$

The corresponding cost is

$$
\mathcal{J}^{*}(T)
=
\sum_{\mu \in \{x,y,z\}}
\left(
\frac{1}{3}\alpha_{\mu}^{2}T^{3}
+
\alpha_{\mu}\beta_{\mu}T^{2}
+
\beta_{\mu}^{2}T
\right)
+
\rho T
$$

The optimal time $T$ is obtained by substituting $\alpha_{\mu}$ and $\beta_{\mu}$ into $\mathcal{J}^{*}(T)$ and finding the roots of

$$
\frac{\partial \mathcal{J}^{*}(T)}{\partial T}=0
$$

The root that gives a minimum cost and a feasible trajectory is selected and denoted as $T_h$. The **heuristic** is

$$
h_c = \mathcal{J}^{*}(T_h)
$$

Finally, $f_c$ is defined as
$$
f_c = g_c + h_c = g_c + \mathcal{J}^{*}(T_h)
$$

##### Kinodynamic Path Searching Algorithm

The searching loop is similar to the standard A* algorithm, where $\mathcal P$ and $\mathcal C$ refer to the open and closed
set. Instead of straight lines, motion primitives respecting the quadrotor dynamic are used as graph edge. A structure $Node$ is used to record a primitive, the voxel the primitives ends in and the $g_c$ and $f_c$ cost.

```pseudocode
Initialize open set P and closed set C;

while P is not empty do
    n_c <- P.pop();
    C.insert(n_c);

    if ReachGoal(n_c) or AnalyticExpand(n_c) then
        return RetrievePath();

    primitives <- Expand(n_c);
    nodes <- Prune(primitives);

    for each n_i in nodes do
        if n_i not in C and CheckFeasible(n_i) then
            g_temp <- n_c.g_c + EdgeCost(n_i);

            if n_i not in P then
                P.add(n_i);
            else if g_temp >= n_i.g_c then
                continue;

            n_i.parent <- n_c;
            n_i.g_c <- g_temp;
            n_i.f_c <- n_i.g_c + Heuristic(n_i);
```

`Expand()` applies discretized control inputs for duration $\tau$ and generates dynamically consistent motion primitives.

 `Prune()` removes primitives ending in the same voxel except the one with the smallest $f_c$. 

`CheckFeasible()` checks safety and dynamic feasibility of the remained primitives.

 `AnalyticExpand()` directly connects the current state $\boldsymbol{x}_c$ to the goal state $\boldsymbol{x}_g$ using the closed-form trajectory above; if the connection is safe and dynamically feasible, the search terminates early.

##### Analytic Expansion

Due to the discretized control input, a motion primitive can hardly end exactly at the goal state. 

Therefore, an analytic expansion is used to speed up the search. When a node is popped from the open set, a closed-form trajectory from $\boldsymbol{x}_c$ to $\boldsymbol{x}_g$ is computed. If it is safe and dynamically feasible, the search terminates in advance. 

This strategy is especially effective in sparse environments.

##### Optimality and Completeness

For optimality, the evaluation shows that the sacrifice of optimality is acceptable and adjustable. Moreover, if the initial path lies near the optimum, the following B-spline optimization can further improve the trajectory and find that optimum.

For completeness, the evaluation indicates that the method can find a feasible solution in most cases. The method can also be extended to support variable-duration primitives and a variable-resolution voxel grid, which can provide stronger completeness guarantees.

##### PMP Derivation

**Pontryagin's Minimum Principle (PMP)** gives **necessary conditions** that an optimal control input and its corresponding state trajectory must satisfy.

In other words, PMP does **not** directly give the final optimal trajectory by itself. Instead, it defines the mathematical conditions that any optimal solution must obey.

For the double-integrator model, the system dynamics along each axis are
$$
\dot{p}_\mu(t)=v_\mu(t)
\\
\dot{v}_\mu(t)=u_\mu(t)
$$

PMP introduces an auxiliary variable called the **costate variables** $\lambda_p(t)$ and $\lambda_v(t)$, and introduces an auxiliary function **Hamiltonian**.

The **Hamiltonian** combines the **running cost** and the **system dynamics** into one expression. The running cost describes what we want to minimize at each time, while the system dynamics describe how the state evolves under the control input.

Here,  the dynamics are
$$
\begin{bmatrix}
v_\mu(t) \\
u_\mu(t)
\end{bmatrix}
$$

the running cost is

$$
u_\mu^2
$$

and the costate vector is

$$
\lambda(t)
=
\begin{bmatrix}
\lambda_p(t) \\
\lambda_v(t)
\end{bmatrix}
$$

then the Hamiltonian is
$$
H
=
u_\mu^2
+
\begin{bmatrix}
\lambda_p & \lambda_v
\end{bmatrix}
\begin{bmatrix}
v_\mu \\
u_\mu
\end{bmatrix}
=
u_\mu^2
+
\lambda_p v_\mu
+
\lambda_v u_\mu
$$
The optimal control input should minimize the Hamiltonian, so it satisfies
$$
\frac{\partial H}{\partial u_\mu}
=
2u_\mu
+
\lambda_v
=
0
$$

Therefore,

$$
u_\mu^*(t)
=
-\frac{1}{2}\lambda_v(t)
$$

The costate equations are

$$
\dot{\lambda}_p
=
-\frac{\partial H}{\partial p_\mu}
=
0
$$

$$
\dot{\lambda}_v
=
-\frac{\partial H}{\partial v_\mu}
=
-\lambda_p
$$

Since

$$
\dot{\lambda}_p=0
$$

we know that $\lambda_p$ is a constant. Let

$$
\lambda_p=c_1
$$

Then

$$
\dot{\lambda}_v=-c_1
$$

so $\lambda_v(t)$ must be a linear function of time

$$
\lambda_v(t)
=
-c_1t+c_2
$$

Substituting this into the optimal control expression gives

$$
u_\mu^*(t)
=
-\frac{1}{2}(-c_1t+c_2)
=
\frac{c_1}{2}t
-
\frac{c_2}{2}
$$

By defining

$$
\alpha_\mu=\frac{c_1}{2},
\beta_\mu=-\frac{c_2}{2}
$$

the optimal control input along each axis has a linear form for the double-integrator system

$$
u_\mu^*(t)
=
\alpha_\mu t
+
\beta_\mu.
$$

Since
$$
u_\mu^*(t)
=
\ddot{p}_\mu^*(t)
$$

integrating the control input twice gives the optimal position trajectory

$$
p_\mu^*(t)
=
\frac{1}{6}\alpha_\mu t^3
+
\frac{1}{2}\beta_\mu t^2
+
v_{\mu c}t
+
p_{\mu c}
$$

Here, $p_{\mu c}$ and $v_{\mu c}$ are the current position and velocity along axis $\mu$.

The coefficients $\alpha_\mu$ and $\beta_\mu$ are determined by enforcing the goal position and velocity constraints at time $T$
$$
p_\mu^*(T)=p_{\mu g}
\\
\dot{p}_\mu^*(T)=v_{\mu g}
$$

Taking the derivative of $p_\mu^*(t)$ gives

$$
\dot{p}_\mu^*(t)
=
\frac{1}{2}\alpha_\mu t^2
+
\beta_\mu t
+
v_{\mu c}
$$

Substituting $t=T$ into the position and velocity equations gives

$$
\frac{1}{6}\alpha_\mu T^3
+
\frac{1}{2}\beta_\mu T^2
=
p_{\mu g}
-
p_{\mu c}
-
v_{\mu c}T
$$

$$
\frac{1}{2}\alpha_\mu T^2
+
\beta_\mu T
=
v_{\mu g}
-
v_{\mu c}
$$

Therefore, $\alpha_\mu$ and $\beta_\mu$ can be solved from the following linear system

$$
\begin{bmatrix}
\frac{1}{6}T^3 & \frac{1}{2}T^2 \\
\frac{1}{2}T^2 & T
\end{bmatrix}
\begin{bmatrix}
\alpha_\mu \\
\beta_\mu
\end{bmatrix}
=
\begin{bmatrix}
p_{\mu g}-p_{\mu c}-v_{\mu c}T \\
v_{\mu g}-v_{\mu c}
\end{bmatrix}
$$

**A closed-form trajectory minimizing** $\mathcal{J}(T)$ from the current state $\boldsymbol{x}_c$ to the goal state $\boldsymbol{x}_g$ is computed by applying PMP.
$$
\begin{bmatrix}
\alpha_{\mu} \\
\beta_{\mu}
\end{bmatrix}
=
\frac{1}{T^3}
\begin{bmatrix}
-12 & 6T \\
6T & -2T^2
\end{bmatrix}
\begin{bmatrix}
p_{\mu g} - p_{\mu c} - v_{\mu c}T \\
v_{\mu g} - v_{\mu c}
\end{bmatrix}
$$

For 3-D motion, the **control cost** is

$$
\int_0^T
\|\boldsymbol{u}(t)\|^2
\,dt
=
\sum_{\mu \in \{x,y,z\}}
\int_0^T
(\alpha_\mu t+\beta_\mu)^2
\,dt
$$

$$
\int_0^T
(\alpha_\mu^2 t^2 + 2\alpha_\mu\beta_\mu t + \beta_\mu^2 )
\,dt
=
\frac{1}{3}\alpha_\mu^2T^3 + \alpha_\mu\beta_\mu T^2 + \beta_\mu^2T
$$

The corresponding cost is
$$
\mathcal{J}^{*}(T)
=
\sum_{\mu \in \{x,y,z\}}
\left(
\frac{1}{3}\alpha_{\mu}^{2}T^{3}
+
\alpha_{\mu}\beta_{\mu}T^{2}
+
\beta_{\mu}^{2}T
\right)
+
\rho T
$$

### B-spline Trajectory Optimization

##### Uniform B-spline

A B-spline is a piecewise polynomial uniquely determined by its degree $p_b$, a set of $N+1$ control points

$$
\{\boldsymbol{Q}_0, \boldsymbol{Q}_1, \cdots, \boldsymbol{Q}_N\},
\qquad
\boldsymbol{Q}_i \in \mathbb{R}^3,
$$

and a knot vector

$$
[t_0,t_1,\cdots,t_M],\qquad
M = N + p_b + 1
$$

The trajectory is parameterized by time

$$
t \in [t_{p_b}, t_{M-p_b}]
$$

For a uniform B-spline, every knot span has the same value

$$
\Delta t_m = t_{m+1} - t_m = \Delta t
$$

For $t \in [t_m,t_{m+1}) \subset [t_{p_b},t_{M-p_b}]$, normalize time by

$$
s(t)=\frac{t-t_m}{\Delta t}
$$

The position is evaluated by the matrix representation

$$
\boldsymbol{p}(s(t)) = \boldsymbol{s}(t)^{T} \mathbf{M}_{p_b+1}\mathbf{q}_m
$$

$$
\boldsymbol{s}(t)=
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & s(t) & s^2(t) & \cdots & s^{p_b}(t)
\end{bmatrix}^{T}
$$

$$
\mathbf{q}_m=
\begin{bmatrix}
\boldsymbol{Q}_{m-p_b} & \boldsymbol{Q}_{m-p_b+1} & \boldsymbol{Q}_{m-p_b+2} & \cdots & \boldsymbol{Q}_m
\end{bmatrix}^{T}
$$

Here $\mathbf{M}_{p_b+1}$ is a constant matrix determined by $p_b$. The derivative of a B-spline is also a B-spline, so derivatives can be evaluated in the same form.

##### Convex Hull Property

The convex hull property is used to ensure both dynamic feasibility and safety. For dynamic feasibility, it is sufficient to constrain all velocity and acceleration control points:

$$
\boldsymbol{V}_i \in [-v_{\max},v_{\max}]^3
\\
\boldsymbol{A}_i \in [-a_{\max},a_{\max}]^3
$$

For the uniform B-spline used in the paper, the derivative control points are computed as

$$
\boldsymbol{V}_i = \frac{1}{\Delta t}(\boldsymbol{Q}_{i+1}-\boldsymbol{Q}_i)
\\
\boldsymbol{A}_i = \frac{1}{\Delta t}(\boldsymbol{V}_{i+1}-\boldsymbol{V}_i)
$$

For safety, every convex hull of the B-spline should be collision-free. Let $d_h$ be the distance between an occupied voxel and any point $\boldsymbol{Q}_h$ in the convex hull. Let $d_c$ be the distance between the occupied voxel and a control point. If $r_{12}, r_{23}, r_{34}$ denote distances between adjacent control points in a cubic B-spline convex hull, then the triangle inequality gives

$$
d_h > d_c - r_h
\\
r_h \le r_{12}+r_{23}+r_{34}
$$

Therefore,

$$
d_h > d_c - (r_{12}+r_{23}+r_{34})
$$

If the following condition is enforced,

$$
d_c > 0
\\
r_{j,j+1} < \frac{d_c}{3},
\qquad
j \in \{1,2,3\}
$$

then the convex hull is guaranteed to be collision-free.

##### Problem Formulation

For a $p_b$ degree B-spline trajectory defined by control points $\{\boldsymbol{Q}_0,\boldsymbol{Q}_1,\cdots,\boldsymbol{Q}_N\}$, the optimization variables are the interior control points

$$
\{\boldsymbol{Q}_{p_b}, \boldsymbol{Q}_{p_b+1}, \cdots, \boldsymbol{Q}_{N-p_b}\}
$$

The first and last $p_b$ control points are fixed because they determine the boundary state. The total cost is

$$
f_{total} = \lambda_1 f_s + \lambda_2 f_c + \lambda_3(f_v + f_a)
$$

where $f_s$ is the smoothness cost, $f_c$ is the collision cost, and $f_v$, $f_a$ are soft limits on velocity and acceleration.

The smoothness cost captures the geometric information of the trajectory and does not depend on time allocation. It is formulated as an elastic band cost

$$
f_s =
\sum_{i=p_b-1}^{N-p_b+1}
\left\|
\underbrace{(\boldsymbol{Q}_{i+1}-\boldsymbol{Q}_i)}_{\boldsymbol{F}_{i+1,i}}
+
\underbrace{(\boldsymbol{Q}_{i-1}-\boldsymbol{Q}_i)}_{\boldsymbol{F}_{i-1,i}}
\right\|^2
$$

This views the trajectory as an elastic band, where $\boldsymbol{F}_{i+1,i}$ and $\boldsymbol{F}_{i-1,i}$ are joint forces of two springs connected to $\boldsymbol{Q}_i$. If all terms are zero, the control points are uniformly distributed in a straight line.

The collision cost is formulated as the repulsive force of obstacles acting on each control point

$$
f_c = \sum_{i=p_b}^{N-p_b} F_c(d(\boldsymbol{Q}_i))
$$

where $d(\boldsymbol{Q}_i)$ is the distance between $\boldsymbol{Q}_i$ and the closest obstacle. The differentiable potential cost function is

$$
F_c(d(\boldsymbol{Q}_i)) =
\begin{cases}
(d(\boldsymbol{Q}_i)-d_{thr})^2 & d(\boldsymbol{Q}_i) \le d_{thr} \\
0 & d(\boldsymbol{Q}_i) > d_{thr}
\end{cases}
$$

The Euclidean distance field provides both $d(\boldsymbol{Q}_i)$ and the gradient direction. For gradient-based optimization, the collision gradient is

$$
\nabla_{\boldsymbol{Q}_i}F_c
=
\begin{cases}
2(d(\boldsymbol{Q}_i)-d_{thr})\nabla d(\boldsymbol{Q}_i) & d(\boldsymbol{Q}_i) \le d_{thr} \\
0 & d(\boldsymbol{Q}_i) > d_{thr}
\end{cases}
$$

Velocity and acceleration are penalized when they exceed the maximum allowable values. For one-dimensional velocity $v_{\mu}$, where $\mu \in \{x,y,z\}$,

$$
F_v(v_{\mu}) =
\begin{cases}
(v_{\mu}^2-v_{\max}^2)^2 & v_{\mu}^2 > v_{\max}^2 \\
0 & v_{\mu}^2 \le v_{\max}^2
\end{cases}
$$

The acceleration penalty $F_a$ has the same form. Using the convex hull property, the paper defines

$$
f_v =
\sum_{\mu \in \{x,y,z\}}
\sum_{i=p_b-1}^{N-p_b}
F_v(V_{i\mu})
$$

$$
f_a =
\sum_{\mu \in \{x,y,z\}}
\sum_{i=p_b-2}^{N-p_b}
F_a(A_{i\mu})
$$

### Time Adjustment

##### Non-uniform B-spline

A non-uniform B-spline is a more general B-spline where each knot span

$$
\Delta t_m = t_{m+1}-t_m
$$

is independent. The first- and second-order derivative control points of a non-uniform B-spline are

$$
\boldsymbol{V}_i' =
\frac{p_b(\boldsymbol{Q}_{i+1}-\boldsymbol{Q}_i)}{t_{i+p_b+1}-t_{i+1}}
$$

$$
\boldsymbol{A}_i' =
\frac{(p_b-1)(\boldsymbol{V}_{i+1}'-\boldsymbol{V}_i')}{t_{i+p_b+1}-t_{i+2}}
$$

By the convex hull property, enforcing all first- and second-order derivative control points within the feasible domain is sufficient to enforce dynamic feasibility of the whole trajectory.

##### Velocity Knot Span Adjustment

Let

$$
\boldsymbol{V}_i' =
\begin{bmatrix}
V_{i,x}' & V_{i,y}' & V_{i,z}'
\end{bmatrix}^{T}
$$

be an infeasible velocity control point. Let $V_{i,\mu}'$ be the largest infeasible component and

$$
|V_{i,\mu}'| = v_m
$$

From the derivative formula, $V_{i,\mu}'$ is influenced by the duration $t_{i+p_b+1}-t_{i+1}$. If this duration is changed to

$$
\hat{t}_{i+p_b+1}-\hat{t}_{i+1}
=
\mu_v(t_{i+p_b+1}-t_{i+1})
$$

then

$$
\hat{V}_{i,\mu}
=
\frac{p_b}{\hat{t}_{i+p_b+1}-\hat{t}_{i+1}}(Q_{i+1,\mu}-Q_{i,\mu})
=
\frac{1}{\mu_v}V_{i,\mu}'
$$

Setting

$$
\mu_v = \frac{v_m}{v_{\max}}
$$

makes the velocity component feasible because

$$
|\hat{V}_{i,\mu}|
=
\frac{v_{\max}}{v_m}|V_{i,\mu}'|
=
v_{\max}
\in [-v_{\max},v_{\max}]
$$

##### Acceleration Knot Span Adjustment

For acceleration, $A_{i,\mu}'$ is influenced by the knot spans associated with $t_{i+p_b+2}-t_{i+1}$ because it is coupled with $V_{i,\mu}'$ and $V_{i+1,\mu}'$. Change

$$
\Delta t_m = t_{m+1}-t_m
$$

to

$$
\Delta \hat{t}_m = \mu_a \Delta t_m,
\qquad
m \in \{i+1,i+2,\cdots,i+p_b+1\}
$$

Then

$$
\hat{A}_{i,\mu}
=
\frac{p_b-1}{\hat{t}_{i+p_b+1}-\hat{t}_{i+2}}
(\hat{V}_{i+1,\mu}-\hat{V}_{i,\mu})
=
\frac{1}{\mu_a^2}A_{i,\mu}'
$$

Let

$$
\mu_a = \left(\frac{a_m}{a_{\max}}\right)^{\frac{1}{2}}
$$

then

$$
|\hat{A}_{i,\mu}|
=
\frac{a_{\max}}{a_m}|A_{i,\mu}'|
=
a_{\max}
\in [-a_{\max},a_{\max}]
$$

##### Iterative Time Adjustment Algorithm

```pseudocode
repeat
    V, A <- findInfeasible();

    for each V_i_prime in V do
        v_m <- max_{mu in {x,y,z}} |V_{i,mu}_prime|;
        mu_v_prime <- min{alpha_v, v_m / v_max};
        AdjustKnotSpansVel(V_i_prime, mu_v_prime);

    for each A_i_prime in A do
        a_m <- max_{mu in {x,y,z}} |A_{i,mu}_prime|;
        mu_a_prime <- min{alpha_a, (a_m / a_max)^{1/2}};
        AdjustKnotSpansAcc(A_i_prime, mu_a_prime);

until V is empty and A is empty
```

The constants $\alpha_v$ and $\alpha_a$ are slightly larger than $1$. They prevent any knot span from being extended excessively in a single iteration, because one knot span influences multiple derivative control points and one derivative control point is also affected by multiple knot spans.
